SUMMARY:
DOE Critical Materials Assessment evaluates materials that are important to clean energy technologies and vulnerable to supply risk. It is relevant because energy resilience, semiconductor manufacturing, advanced batteries, magnets, and defense-adjacent systems share upstream material dependencies. The source helps a RAG assistant distinguish criticality from scarcity: a material becomes critical when both importance and supply risk are high. It can be paired with IEA and USGS sources for a fuller view of demand, production, and policy response.

USEFUL_PARAPHRASED_TEXT_FOR_INGESTION:
This source should be used to define material criticality. A material can be abundant in the earth but still critical if processing is concentrated, substitution is difficult, demand is rising, or supply disruption would affect essential technologies. A RAG assistant should state the criteria instead of only listing minerals.
The assessment is valuable for connecting clean energy, industrial base, and technology competition. Batteries, electric motors, grid equipment, solar components, and power electronics all depend on specialized inputs. Some risks can be reduced through recycling, substitution, domestic production, stockpiling, or allied sourcing, but each mitigation has cost and time limits.
For contradiction testing, pair DOE with IEA and USGS. If sources disagree on criticality, the assistant should explain that different methodologies, time horizons, and technology assumptions can produce different rankings.
