SUMMARY:
This CSIS analysis examines whether U.S. allies have the legal tools and political capacity to implement AI and semiconductor export controls similar to U.S. measures. It argues that unilateral U.S. controls are limited because allies control important semiconductor chokepoints, including equipment, materials, and manufacturing capacity. The report compares authorities in the EU, Netherlands, Germany, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China. It notes that allies may not have direct equivalents to U.S. tools like the Entity List or foreign direct product rule, but may still be able to implement controls through other mechanisms. The source is useful for explaining why coalition alignment matters. It is also useful for testing contradictions between U.S. policy ambition and allied legal or political constraints.

USEFUL_PARAPHRASED_TEXT_FOR_INGESTION:
This source addresses the coalition problem in semiconductor export controls. A RAG system should extract the idea that the United States cannot fully control the advanced semiconductor value chain alone. Important chokepoints are held by allies and partners, including the Netherlands, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Germany, and the European Union. These actors may control equipment, materials, production capacity, or regulatory authority needed to make U.S. technology-denial strategies effective.

The report explains that allied legal systems differ from U.S. law. The United States has tools such as the Export Administration Regulations, Entity List, and foreign direct product rule. Some allies may not have exact equivalents, or they may require different procedures, thresholds, or political approvals. A lack of perfect alignment with U.S. policy does not always mean an ally lacks legal authority; it may reflect enforcement capacity, economic exposure, political willingness, or a different risk assessment.
